> **Question**: What is the legacy of Richard Aoki? I've heard that he was a founding member of AAPA and other orgs like the Black Panthers, but I also heard that he was an informant for the FBI. How should we feel about his legacy, and how should we treat folks who face these kinds of accusations generally?

# His work

Richard Aoki was one of the most controversial figures of the movement. You either loved him or you hated him. He never let you off the spot, and he would put you up against the wall to get you to tell him what you were about!

Richard Aoki helped found multiple organizations: the [[Black Panther Party]], [[AAPA]], the [[TWLF]], [[Asian Studies]], and Ethnic Studies.

He lived his life serving the people and educating the masses. He learned the power of organization and self-defense, starting a street gang in West Oakland. From there, he learn how to protect himself and his friends from getting beat up and bullied.

He always knew about the situation of everyone in the neighborhood. He would bail people out of prison and do political education out on the street. He pointed to capitalism and imperialism as the reason for the world's ills.

Richard Aoki was always concerned with making sure folks knew how they would get things done. He always asked: "Could you really explain what you were talking about, how to carry it out?" During the [[TWLF]] strikes, he would always mention: doing a strike isn't hard. Building a department—something that lasts—is much harder.

He wasn't perfect. He was prone to anger, and on more than one occasion we had to tell him: "you can't kick his butt, he's our friend!" and "you've had a lot to drink!" But, *we know who he was by how he lived his life*. The movement always came before himself. And he would always tell us: "loose lips sink ships."

Later on, he helped found the first field office around the corner of [[I-Hotel]]. After this was taking away, he helped establish the [[Asian Community Center]], along with [[Everybody's Bookstore]] (also around the corners). All these social services grew out of this!

# Was he an informant?

In 2012, Richard Aoki was alleged to be an FBI informant by an investigative reporter. More information from [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Aoki#Posthumous_revelation_as_an_FBI_informant):

> On August 20, 2012, a report by [Center for Investigative Reporting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_for_Investigative_Reporting "Center for Investigative Reporting") journalist [Seth Rosenfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seth_Rosenfeld "Seth Rosenfeld") alleged Aoki was an [FBI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FBI "FBI") informant who had infiltrated chapters of the [Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communist_Party_USA "Communist Party USA"), the [Socialist Workers' Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Workers_Party_\(United_States\) "Socialist Workers Party (United States)") and, nearly from its inception, the [Black Panther Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Panther_Party "Black Panther Party"). In response to a [FOIA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freedom_of_Information_Act_\(United_States\) "Freedom of Information Act (United States)") request by Rosenfeld, it was revealed that a November 16, 1967, FBI intelligence report listed Aoki as an informant with the code number "T-2". Former FBI agent turned banker, Burney Threadgill Jr., also said that he worked with Aoki, stating, "He was my informant. I developed him."
>
> On September 7, 2012, the Center for Investigative Reporting published a second story about Aoki with new documents detailing his 221-page informant file. The file was released under court order after a Freedom of Information Act lawsuit. The second story notes Aoki was designated the code name "Richard Ford". The file details 16 years of cooperation between Aoki and the FBI's San Francisco office. According to the story, the records show "that at various points, he provided information that was 'unique' and of 'extreme value.'"

## Vicci's perspective

There are multiple reasons to believe that this is an untrue allegation.

For one, many people have personal experience organizing with him! There are many accounts from folks who have personally worked with him over years; nobody who has worked personally with him has ever said anything negative about him. He was constantly involved in local community work and, for many folks, he was the person who helped involve them in politics and radicalized them through his political education efforts. He was personally involved in giving people legal counsel, bailing people out of jail, getting them connected to community organizations.

On the other hand, there are reasons to believe that the account of the journalist, Seth Rosenfeld, isn't entirely accurate. There are three sources that he's using to support his claim:

1. **FOIA requested documents**. These documents could have been doctored, and there's no reason to believe that these documents in this form is 100% accurate. Notably, it's bizarre that these documents explicitly mention Richard Aoki by name, given that it's a common rule in intelligence circles to never name sources. While these documents were evaluated by another FBI agent involved with COINTELPRO to "seem to look correct," this agent has no connection to this particular case and didn't have any particular information about it beyond what any other observer would have.
2. **Richard Aoki's "handler"**. Rosenfeld's claim is supported by interviews with Burney Threadgill Jr., the FBI agent who allegedly "developed him." Again, the number one rule of intelligence agencies is to never reveal sources, and so it's strange that this would be the case.
3. **Richard Aoki himself**. Conveniently, at the time that Rosenfeld conducted this investigation, Aoki had already passed, and so he was unable to defend himself or dispute these allegations.

Given these things, why would we choose to believe the person who doesn't have personal connections to the situation, and whose sources are largely conveniently unavailable?

Second, we should consider the broader political context under which these allegations were made. As these allegations were made, Occupy Wall Street was happening in the background—a historical moment in which folks were turning their attention to capital and capitalism. This article just so happened to occur right as folks were starting to get angry and this and as momentum was building around this movement.

And indeed, why would someone want to publish something like this? It's to discredit the movement and undermine organizer histories—to turn organizers against each other, because if Richard Aoki was an informant, then what was it all for?

## Sifu Fong's perspective

The history of Richard Aoki is more complex than it might seem!

First of all, he was cunning, and was able to play folks against each other. One of the stories he told: two competing ninja organizations during Tokigawa period in Japan. The one person who was the leader of each of these rival orgs was hired by diff masters to assassinate folks and make political change. Turns out, nobody knew until master died that he commanded both organizations, pitting each other against one another! Richard knew how to play games, both sides of the coin... Getting info out of them that normally they wouldn't tell you

Much of the information that he was revealed to be passing on was information that was (i) already publicly available and (ii) not information that folks assumed would be private to begin with. Folks in the movement were aware that their phones were being tapped and that a dedicated observer could figure out ways of listening in on their conversations, and so they behaved accordingly.

Moreover, Richard wasn't a leader in the usual sense; he wanted *you* to be out front, to train the people. For instance, at an action, Richard pointed out who the undercover cops were, and would make plans: "If anything happens, I'll take out the guy on the right, you take the guy on the left."

Another story; during the TWLF strikes, Richard and Sifu Fong saw five cops coming to get them!! Richard says: "can you go over there for a minute!" He then jumps the cops and gets arrested. At the trial, Richard then says he was protecting his students. Because he was so much smaller than the five policemen, he got acquitted!
